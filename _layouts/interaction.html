Interaction design

bodypaint (2016) is an interactive methodology for learning the fundamentals of computational thinking and programming through the expression of gestures in the body and their algorithmic response, which participants can modify and play with, understanding the concept of conditionals in computer science.

I use interaction design to explore how digital interfaces and technologies translate, represent and mediate knowledge about the world. Exploring the translatability between systems, their ranges and their protocols, conventions and symbols, I seek to engage participants in expoloring how those translations operate, and ultimately how they can be defined, hacked and created from scratch, with the purpose to inquire what this means in the politics of who decides the operability and what is possible between interfaces, information, and us as agents in these processes of meaning production.

[des]encuentros (2015) (transl. [mis]encounters) is an intervention design for a theater, originally designed for Espacio Odeón in Bogotá, Colombia, a 1940's modernist theater in ruins now turned contemporary art exhibition and research center. The piece inquires around the concept of the digital representation of bodies and identities and how their encounter or dissolution in digital space resolves in the ephemeral or the transcendent.

Colombia 3.0 event's colourful pathways is a simple live coding collaboration where I intervened the main hall's floors so that people could play with the pathways they left behind. (2015)

Getting curious with Kinect (2015) was a simple intervention for the the Colectivo Estratósfera's Fiesta Interplatanaria electronic party in Manizales, Colombia where participants were invited to get curious about the fundamentals of programming and interactivity through dance and movement.

Doves (2015) was a 12 hour challenge to intervene one the the hallways of a public school during a presidential visit to Manizales, Colombia.

Surprise Museum (2014) was a short excercise intervention at Los Andes University in Bogota where famous cubist paintings were projected as if they were hanging on the wall for the passers-by to discover that they could actually play with them via their shadow.
